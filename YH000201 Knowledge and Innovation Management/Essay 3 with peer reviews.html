Organizations increasingly face the issue of "copyright infringement," as material protected by a copyright might be shared without authorization on a small to large scale within another organization (Arai, 2014). When users of YouTube uploaded Viacom-owned copyrighted content, such as television episodes and excerpts, on the platform without authorization, Viacom International, Inc. filed a lawsuit against YouTube (Hassanabadi, 2011). According to Viacom, YouTube knew about the copyright violation but took no action to stop it (Kumar, 2008), which could impede Viacom’s innovations.


The idea of copyright infringement covered in Lecture 10 sheds light on this issue and explains why successful businesses sometimes struggle to grow despite their best efforts. Intellectual property can be used, copied, distributed, or modified without permission or an appropriate license from the copyright owners (Lemley, 2003). This is known as copyright infringement. Original owners of books, music, art, and other creative works are shielded by copyright laws. Owners are alone in charge of how their work is used, distributed, and made money during the duration of this protection. Economic and moral rights, such as authorship and work integrity, are granted to the owner of the work by copyrights (Menell, 2008).

In cases of copyright infringement problems, several key solutions can be considered. Firstly, it is necessary to educate users, content creators, and platforms on fair use and copyright laws to help them understand the limitations of copyright protection. To automatically detect and eliminate unlawful content for platforms such as YouTube, content monitoring solutions such as Content ID can be used, which can recognize and control copyrighted content on the website (Hassanabadi,2011). This tactic can strike a balance between copyright protection and the expansion of user-generated content. Organizations should encourage content creators and copyright holders to sign licensing agreements, offer compensation and file a lawsuit to protect copyright holders' rights. They should also establish clear community guidelines that penalize and discourage copyright infringement. Combining these approaches reduces infringement issues and promotes an atmosphere that upholds intellectual property rights while fostering innovation and idea exchange (Lemley, 2003) .

References:

  • Hassanabadi, A. (2011). Viacom v. YouTube-All Eyes Blind: The Limits of the DMCA in a Web 2.0 World. Berkeley Tech. LJ, 26, 405.
